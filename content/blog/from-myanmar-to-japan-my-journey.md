My path into software engineering didn't start with a computer. It started with curiosity about how things worked, and a lot of improvisation, growing up as part of the Lisu people in Myanmar.

## Constraints as a teacher

Reliable electricity and internet access weren't guaranteed where I grew up. That sounds like a disadvantage for someone who'd end up writing code for a living, and in some ways it was. But it also meant that every project I touched early on had to be designed around failure as the default state, not the exception. Years later, when colleagues talk about "designing for unreliable networks" as an advanced systems concept, I recognize it as something I learned by necessity long before I had a name for it.

## Finding software by accident, then on purpose

I didn't grow up with a clear path to becoming an engineer. Access to formal computer science education was limited, so most of what I learned came from documentation, forums, and a lot of trial and error on borrowed hardware. That self-taught foundation made me comfortable with ambiguity — when there's no professor or curriculum to tell you the "right" way, you get used to figuring things out from first principles.

## Carrying identity into a new country

Moving from Myanmar to Japan meant navigating two layers of being an outsider — culturally Burmese, but also Lisu within Myanmar itself, a minority within a minority. I've come to see that as an asset rather than something to smooth over. It's given me practice at adapting quickly, listening before assuming, and finding common ground with people whose context is completely different from mine — which, it turns out, describes most days working on a multicultural engineering team in Tokyo.

## Why I share this

Engineering blogs tend to skip the personal context that shapes how someone actually thinks about problems. Mine isn't separate from how I write code or design systems — it's the reason I default to building for constraints instead of assuming ideal conditions.
