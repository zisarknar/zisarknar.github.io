People ask me why a software engineer from Myanmar ends up writing code in Tokyo. The short answer is opportunity. The longer answer is more interesting.

## The decision wasn't really about Japan at first

I wasn't chasing Tokyo specifically — I was chasing a place where I could work on hard engineering problems with a team that pushed me to get better. Japan's tech scene, especially in fintech and infrastructure, turned out to be exactly that. The specifics came later; the direction came first.

## The language barrier is real, and it's not just about words

I studied Japanese before moving, but technical fluency and workplace fluency are different skills entirely. Reading a design document is one thing. Picking up on the unspoken disagreement in a meeting where everyone is being extremely polite is another. I underestimated how much communication in a Japanese engineering team happens between the lines, and I spent the first year mostly listening.

## Being an outsider sharpened my engineering instincts

There's a strange benefit to not sharing every cultural assumption with your team: you ask more "why do we do it this way" questions, because you genuinely don't know the answer. Some of those questions were naive. A few of them turned out to expose process problems nobody had questioned in years.

## What I'd tell my younger self

Don't wait until your language skills feel "ready." You'll improve faster inside a real engineering team with real stakes than in any classroom. And don't underestimate how much your background — growing up navigating limited resources, unreliable infrastructure, and constant problem-solving — is itself an engineering advantage. It taught me to build things that work under constraints, which turns out to be most of the job.
